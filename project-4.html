Overview

ROLE: Learning Engineer

DURATION: Jan 2018 - Jun 2019

CONTRIBUTION:

  • Conducted 50+ semi-structured interviews with teachers and school administrators to identify pain points in current project-based courses
  • Collaborated with Subject Matter Experts to design learning materials, activities, and assessments tailored for high school students, while providing explicit handbooks for teachers as a reference
  • Created visualized student reports with Tableau, enabling teachers and school administrators to easily track student progress and provide personalized support
  • Developed and maintained an e-learning platform for middle and high school students, currently serving over 30 schools

Problem

From the conversion with high school teachers and school administrators, I found current project-based courses have lots of problems...

"Sometimes I don’t know when I should give students instructional intervention."

"I wanna a scientific standard to assess my students’ learning."

"I don't have lots of teaching experience in project-based courses, and I really wanna get an explicit instructor handbook which I can refer to."

"I think tracking student learning is very important, but It’s hard to do just by observation. I wanna a tool to help me record learning and show learning data clearly."

"As a school administrator, I’d like to monitor each class performance and get student reports by classes."

Solution

Drawing from interview insights, I spearheaded the development of the Engineering Design Process (EDP) course, integrating it with the school's learning platform to enable seamless student progress monitoring for teachers and administrators.

This comprehensive solution encompasses two key components:

  • The HeartRate Box Course, designed to equip high school students with essential EDP thinking abilities and application skills, complemented by an explicit handbook to assist teachers in implementation.
  • Implementation of visualized student reports utilizing Tableau, seamlessly embedded within the e-learning platform, empowering teachers and school administrators to effortlessly track student learning and deliver timely, personalized assistance.

Why EDP?

EDP with real-world challenges can effectively enhance open-ended problem-solving skills, which is a crucial ability for high school students to develop.

Project Context

Course Type

High school elective course, Engineering Design Domain, encompassing interdisciplinary knowledge from subjects like math, physics, and biology. This project focuses on the final 10 units of the course.

Learner

High school students who possess foundational knowledge of engineering concepts and have prior experience with the WonderBits Learning Kit.

Community

Consists of students with diverse learning preferences and varying levels of prior knowledge. To cater to their needs, the community is divided into small, complementary ability groups, providing tailored guidance and ample resources.

Belonging and Norms

The sense of belonging and norms are fostered through a shared project goal and self-assessment, which empowers students to take ownership of their projects. The instructor's support, feedback, and guidance further reinforce the value placed on learners' projects and their individual growth.

Learner Profile

Based on further interviews with learners, I conducted an analysis from three aspects: developmental level, prior experience, and individual differences, aiming to gain a deeper understanding of the learners and align their goals with instructional goals.

Developmental Level

  • Age: 15 - 18
  • Demonstrates relatively strong processing abilities, such as identifying and summarizing challenges and solutions in real-world cases.
  • Currently experiencing rapid physical and social development.

Prior Experience

  • Possesses basic interdisciplinary knowledge covered in the Engineering Design Course, including math, physics, and biology.
  • Familiarity with using computers, with varying levels of programming skills.
  • Has basic knowledge of the key modules of the WonderBits learning kit and an understanding of engineering concepts.
  • Some prior experience with project-based learning, but limited exposure to the engineering design process.

Individual Differences

  • Exhibits diverse prior knowledge and interests in engineering-related disciplines.
  • Varies in collaboration skills and working preferences.

What should learners learn?

The instructional goals for learners are designed to meet their needs, align with the school's requirements, and draw from established standards in STEAM fields.

These goals are grounded in sound theoretical foundations and informed by various guidelines and frameworks, such as Infusing Engineering Design into High School STEM Courses, which outlines the 7 steps of the Engineering Design Process (EDP) and serves as a reference for setting instructional goals that encompass the EDP; Engineering Frameworks for a High School Setting: Guidelines for Technical Literacy for High School Students, which provides a good engineering paradigm that enables students to evaluate the functionality and capabilities of products. Aligned with established goals, the instructional goals are classified by conceptual knowledge, procedural skills, disposition and their according meta-level goals.

How can I assess learning?

Assessment is a tool to

“guide decisions about what content needs to be emphasized

versus that which is not really essential”

rather than

a standard to judge students.

To ensure effective assessment and capture evidence of students' progress in meeting the desired learning outcomes, the assessment approach emphasizes the learning process rather than solely focusing on the final outcome. The course incorporates 6 performance tasks that align with the instructional goals: Key questions about EDP, 2 Case studies, mid-project presentation, final presentation and self-assessment by index card after each class.

  • Self-assessment by index card will be conducted after each class.
  • Most feedback will be provided before the next class. The brief feedback of the presentation will be first provided instantly after the in-class presentation.

How can I choose instructional activities?

Creating an environment for students

to struggle with their own thinking,

to express their ideas to others,

and to have their ideas challenged

can significantly improve students’ understanding of key topics

Guided by goal, assessment and instructional principles, I designed the learner-centered instruction and established class norms as well.

Instructional Principles Behind These Activities

  • Provocative questions and challenging problems: By organizing the course around thought-provoking questions and challenging problems, students are motivated to actively engage in the learning process. This approach helps to activate their prior knowledge, sustain their interest, and allows instructors to assess their current knowledge level.
  • Gradual progression: The activities are designed to gradually introduce more specific and complex tasks, such as case studies, after students have gained sufficient experience and familiarity with the subject matter. This sequencing ensures that students are well-prepared to tackle more advanced challenges and encourages deeper learning.
  • Emphasis on connections and context: Lectures can emphasize links within/between concepts and between processes and the contexts of their use. Besides, instructional videos provided after the lecture can help students with relatively lower knowledge levels better understand different functions of various modules.
  • Sharing and discussion: The sharing and discussion are about “ideas, issues, or problems” which can create a connection between the conceptual knowledge and the task that students did, in turn endowing the act of completing a task with deeper meaning than merely handling in an assignment.

The "HeartRate Box" Engineering Design Process Course has been successfully implemented in over 30 high schools, earning me the first prize in the "iTeach" National College Student Digital Education Application Innovation Competition on 12/02/2018.
