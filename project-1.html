Problem

Our target users are Generation Zers, who were born between 1997 and 2012. They make up about 20 percent of the U.S. population and are by far the most educated generation.

From our user research and references, we found that compared with the past generations, Gen Z are more

Target-oriented

They have got their eyes set on success and upskilling instead of only getting satisfied with temporary pleasure of shortcut entertainment;

Community-oriented

They want to connect through shared values and are more interested in helping the wider community.

Struggling with mental health

Gen Z is considered to be the most depressed generation. 91% of Gen Z reported experiencing psychological symptoms due to stress. According to a study by Western Governors University, only 45% of Gen Z believe their mental health is good, which is 11% lower than the millennial generation. The Pew Research Center found that 70% of teens believe that depression and anxiety are significant problems amongst their peers. Gen Z are 25% more likely than everyone else to say thereâ€™s too much pressure to be perfect on people-based social media as well.

Solution

What do Gen Zers want?

According to the generation report, they are seeking a more honest, open, and carefree online environment, where besides funny/light-hearted content, they would like to share information that helps people and browse useful content for self improvement.

Our Solution

Most social media products start from student groups, so with Gen Zers coming to colleges, Gpace community is provided to empower them with learning resources and life advice so they can go above and beyond in their college journey.

Product Structure

On a high level, Gpace is a combination of User Generated Content (UGC) and Professionally Generated Content (PGC).

For UGC, we envision growing Gpace towards a user-generated content centric community to keep the community lively with the active users and the continuous flow of content.

Specifically, as the foundational direction of the Gpace community, we will encourage and feed valuable UGC content to users, and allow users to record their memorable moments during their college journey using a more casual format.

On top of the fragmented UGC content, the Gpace team will provide the Gpace-generated-content within a more systematic and professional format. This part could have the content such as the learning materials that help students to crack the concepts of their courses, the online question bank that can help them to prepare for the exams, and so on.

As time progresses, we envision Gpace will eventually be predominantly carrying useful content contributed by both users and Gpace team, and it will serve as a platform where users can communicate by useful information. When users and non-users think of Gpace, they will think of a platform that is meaningful, enjoyable, reciprocal and professional.

Competitive Analysis

As for Tiktok and Pinterest, one focuses on fun short videos and another focuses on high-quality pictures with shopping links, but they both can do better on providing useful content to college students. Regarding CourseHero and Chegg, they are identified as tooling products, where students can access and exchange course resources. However, the business model of UGC learning resources can result in legal issues, which makes these kinds of platforms constantly operate in a gray area that professors donâ€™t endorse. Gpace, instead of using the learning resources created by professors, the Gpace mode will collect the learning resources, iterate them for the new questions, and then provide them to students, which not only avoid the potential copyright infringement, but also have a potential cooperation opportunity with professors in a win-win situation.
